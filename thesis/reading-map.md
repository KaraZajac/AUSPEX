# Reading map — the literature each thesis claim must engage

**How to use this.** Organized by dissertation claim → the conversation that claim enters →
starting works. **Every citation below is from memory and MUST be independently verified
(title, year, venue) before it appears in any document you submit** — treat entries as search
pointers, not a bibliography. The per-claim "what you must answer" line is the examiner
question the reading prepares you for.

## Claim 1 — "Attribution names the who; doctrine names the why; the join is the product"

*Conversation: attribution theory & practice.*
**You must answer:** what does doctrine-informed attribution add beyond Rid & Buchanan's Q-model
levels, and beyond what vendor analysts already do informally?

- Rid, T. & Buchanan, B. — "Attributing Cyber Attacks" (Journal of Strategic Studies, ~2015).
  The canonical attribution framework; your who×why join either extends or contests its
  strategic layer. Read first.
- Egloff, F. — work on public attribution & semi-state actors (incl. *Semi-State Actors in
  Cybersecurity*); Egloff & Smeets on publicly attributing cyber operations. Directly relevant
  to your defender-response perspective class and counter-op conventions.
- Romanosky, S. (RAND) — on the economics/practice of attribution and vendor reporting quality.
  Relevant to your vendor-consensus-as-ground-truth framing.
- Mandiant APT1 report (2013) — the founding artifact of public vendor attribution; your corpus
  formalizes what it did narratively.
- ODNI ICD 203 / ICD 206 — read the actual standards; your SCHEMA cites them precisely now.
- Heuer, R. — *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis* — analytic-confidence discipline;
  underpins your never-upgrade rule.

## Claim 2 — Doctrine as an operationalizable construct (the `kind` taxonomy)

*Conversation: strategic culture & military doctrine theory.*
**You must answer:** what *is* a doctrine such that a 1946 treaty, a posture, and a five-year
plan can share a label space — and why is that defensible rather than category error?

- Johnston, A.I. — *Cultural Realism* / strategic-culture debates; Gray, C. vs Johnston
  exchange on whether strategic culture is observable in behavior — this IS your
  posture-vs-document problem in IR-theory form.
- Posen, B. — *The Sources of Military Doctrine* — the classic definition; your `kind: strategy`
  class descends from it.
- PLA *Science of Military Strategy* (translations), the Gerasimov article (2013) and the
  literature debunking "the Gerasimov doctrine" (e.g., Galeotti's retraction) — directly
  relevant: your corpus marks Gerasimov as `inferred`/posture, which the literature supports.
- Giles, K. — Russian information warfare doctrine analyses (NATO StratCom / Chatham House).
- Bateman, J. (Carnegie) — Russia's wartime cyber operations — doctrine-vs-practice gaps.

## Claim 3 — Doctrine carries measurable attribution-relevant information (the bits result)

*Conversation: empirical/quantitative cyber-conflict research.*
**You must answer:** how does your corpus differ from existing datasets, and why should anyone
trust entropy measurements on curated event data?

- Valeriano, B. & Maness, R. — *Cyber War versus Cyber Realities* + the Dyadic Cyber Incident
  Dataset (DCID) — the closest academic ancestor; you must position against it explicitly
  (they code dyads/severity; you code doctrine).
- EuRepoC (European Repository of Cyber Incidents) — the closest *living* dataset; compare
  coding schemes, coverage, and what their methodology papers say about reliability.
- CFR Cyber Operations Tracker; CISSM cyber events dataset (Harry & Gallagher) — coverage
  comparisons.
- Maschmeyer, L. — "The Subversive Trilemma" and related — on why cyber ops underdeliver
  strategically; tension with your doctrine-coupling claim worth addressing head-on.
- Work, JD — on commercial threat-intelligence epistemics and source reliability — supports
  your vendor-consensus framing.
- Rennie et al. (2003) — the ComplementNB paper (cite for the engine); Guo et al. (2017) on
  calibration of modern classifiers (temperature scaling).

## Claim 4 — The deterrence non-result ("not identifiable from this design")

*Conversation: cyber-deterrence theory — where your negative finding lands hardest.*
**You must answer:** whose claims does your non-identifiability result discipline, and why is
an honest "cannot be answered observationally" a contribution?

- Libicki, M. — *Cyberdeterrence and Cyberwar* (RAND) — the foundational optimist text.
- Nye, J. — "Deterrence and Dissuasion in Cyberspace" (International Security, ~2017) — the
  taxonomy your null result speaks to.
- Borghard, E. & Lonergan, S. — on the logic (and limits) of coercion/deterrence in cyberspace.
- Healey, J. — on persistent engagement & defend-forward effectiveness debates (your US doctrine
  records cite the primary documents; Healey's assessments are the scholarly layer).
- Fischerkeller & Harknett — persistent engagement / agreed competition — the doctrine your
  corpus tags on US ops; their claims about initiative persistence are testable against your
  data and worth a pointed paragraph.
- Lindsay, J. — on attribution credibility and deterrence (deception/stability work) — bridges
  Claims 1 and 4.
- Gartzke, E. — "The Myth of Cyberwar" — the skeptic baseline.

## Claim 5 — Label reliability & dataset methodology (the inter-rater + verification chapters)

*Conversation: research methods / dataset papers.*
**You must answer:** what are accepted reliability standards for event-coding datasets, and do
you meet them?

- Standard content-analysis reliability texts (Krippendorff — *Content Analysis*, the alpha
  coefficient; Cohen's kappa literature) — your inter-rater study should cite the standard it
  applies.
- The methodology/codebook papers of EuRepoC and DCID — what reliability did *they* report?
  (This sets the bar you must clear or exceed.)
- GTD (Global Terrorism Database) codebook + critiques — the closest mature analog of a
  long-running curated event dataset and its known biases; reviewers will reach for this
  comparison.
- Acceptance-sampling / data-quality literature for the T3 sampling design in
  docs/CORPUS-VERIFICATION-PLAN.md.

## Claim 6 — Forecasting & the geopolitical calendar (if kept in scope)

- Literature on cyber operations tempo & geopolitical events (e.g., work around election-period
  targeting, conflict-onset cyber activity — Kostyuk & Zhukov on cyber and battlefield events
  is the key skeptical empirical result to engage).

## Suggested reading order (first pass ≈ 6–8 weeks alongside verification work)

1. Rid & Buchanan; Valeriano & Maness; EuRepoC methodology — the spine (Claims 1+3).
2. Posen; Johnston/Gray exchange — the construct chapter (Claim 2).
3. Libicki; Nye; Fischerkeller & Harknett; Kostyuk & Zhukov — the deterrence chapter (Claim 4).
4. Krippendorff + the EuRepoC/DCID codebooks — methods (Claim 5).
5. Everything else as the chapters demand.

*Keep a per-source note file (`thesis/notes/<author-year>.md`): 3 bullets — what it claims,
what you take from it, where your work agrees/contests. Those notes become the literature
review's paragraphs.*
