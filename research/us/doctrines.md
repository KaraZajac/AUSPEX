# United States — Cyber Doctrines

US cyber doctrine is unusually public. The entries below cite primary documents wherever they exist; classified pieces (NSPM-13) are noted as such with the best public reporting.

---

### National Cybersecurity Strategy (2023) and Implementation Plan (2023, 2024)

- **Issued**: March 2, 2023 by The White House (Biden administration, drafted by ONCD)
- **Official text**: https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/National-Cybersecurity-Strategy-2023.pdf
- **Implementation Plan v1**: July 13, 2023 — 65 initiatives — https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/National-Cybersecurity-Strategy-Implementation-Plan-WH.gov_.pdf
- **Implementation Plan v2**: May 7, 2024 — 100 initiatives, 18 agencies — https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/National-Cybersecurity-Strategy-Implementation-Plan-Version-2.pdf
- **Status**: superseded in framing by Trump's March 2026 "Cyber Strategy for America" but most implementation continues
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  1. Defend critical infrastructure (sector regulators set minimum requirements)
  2. Disrupt and dismantle threat actors (sustained offensive tempo; ransomware as national-security threat)
  3. Shape market forces to drive security and resilience (liability shift to software producers)
  4. Invest in a resilient future (post-quantum, zero-trust federal architecture)
  5. Forge international partnerships (counter-ransomware initiative, allied capacity)
- **Sectors / targets implied**: water, healthcare, ICS/OT, software supply chain, federal civilian executive branch (FCEB) networks
- **Timeline pressure**: post-Colonial Pipeline (2021) political demand; CIRCIA implementation deadlines; 2024 election security
- **Summary**: The defining strategic document of the Biden cyber era. Two structural shifts — burden onto large capable actors (vendors, infrastructure operators) and longer-time-horizon investment — operationalized via Pillar 2's explicit endorsement of *disruption* (CYBERCOM, FBI, IC working in concert against criminal and state actors) and Pillar 3's liability framework. Underpins Secure-by-Design, the OS3I, the CIRCIA rule, and harmonization efforts.
- **Citations**: [1] White House 2023 strategy PDF. [2] ONCD Implementation Plan v2 fact sheet, May 7, 2024. [3] Lawfare analysis (Daniel/Wallace). [4] Mayer Brown IP v2 summary.

### Trump Cyber Strategy for America (March 2026) and EO 14306 (June 2025)

- **Issued**: EO 14306 "Sustaining Select Efforts to Strengthen the Nation's Cybersecurity..." June 6, 2025; Cyber Strategy for America fact sheet March 6, 2026
- **Official text**: https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/06/sustaining-select-efforts-to-strengthen-the-nations-cybersecurity-and-amending-executive-order-13694-and-executive-order-14144/
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: private-sector-led offensive partnership ("centers private sector in offensive cyber operations" per Lawfare); focus on cybercrime/fraud; software supply chain via NIST consortium; sanctions narrowed to "foreign persons"
- **What it removed from the Biden EO 14144**: the CISA/ONCD deeper-scrutiny mandate on software self-attestations; the phishing-resistant-authentication directive for federal agencies
- **Summary**: Trump 2.0's cyber framing keeps the offensive tempo and supply-chain hardening but rolls back regulatory mechanisms and reorients sanctions/policy toward foreign-actor focus and consumer-fraud emphasis. The Cyberspace Solarium Commission 2.0 2025 report warns that ~24% of previously-implemented Solarium recommendations have *lost* implemented status — an "unprecedented setback."
- **Citations**: [1] EO 14306 (whitehouse.gov). [2] White House fact sheet on Cyber Strategy for America (March 6, 2026). [3] Lawfare "Trump Admin Cyber Strategy Centers Private Sector." [4] CSC 2.0 2025 Annual Report.

### 2023 DoD Cyber Strategy

- **Issued**: classified version May 2023; unclassified summary September 12, 2023 by Department of Defense
- **Official text**: https://media.defense.gov/2023/Sep/12/2003299076/-1/-1/1/2023_DOD_Cyber_Strategy_Summary.pdf
- **Status**: active (supersedes 2018 DoD Cyber Strategy)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: defend the nation; prepare to fight and win the nation's wars; protect the cyber domain with allies and partners; build enduring advantages in cyberspace
- **Sectors / targets implied**: adversary malicious cyber ecosystems (cybercrime services, bulletproof hosters, malware developers); adversary military C2; DIB
- **Timeline pressure**: Russia–Ukraine war lessons; PRC pre-positioning campaigns (Volt Typhoon); allied capacity scaling
- **Summary**: Folds **Defend Forward** and **Persistent Engagement** into the broader DoD construct of "campaigning." Explicit shift to *cyber realism* (per Healey/War on the Rocks): deterrence by denial and disruption rather than deterrence by punishment. The strategy is grounded in real-world experience since 2018 — 90+ Hunt Forward-style operations — and acknowledges cyber's limited standalone strategic effect outside combined campaigns.
- **Citations**: [1] DoD 2023 Cyber Strategy Summary (defense.gov). [2] War on the Rocks "Welcome to Cyber Realism" Sept 2023. [3] Lawfare commentary by Erica Lonergan / Jacquelyn Schneider. [4] DefenseScoop coverage.

### Defend Forward and Persistent Engagement (CYBERCOM 2018 Command Vision)

- **Issued**: 2018 — "Achieve and Maintain Cyberspace Superiority: Command Vision for US Cyber Command"; reaffirmed in DoD Cyber Strategy 2018 and 2023
- **Official text**: https://www.cybercom.mil/Media/News/Article/3198878/cyber-101-defend-forward-and-persistent-engagement/ (explainer); 2018 Command Vision PDF on cybercom.mil
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: continuous contact with adversaries below the threshold of armed conflict; disruption at the source; integration of intelligence and operations
- **Summary**: The intellectual revolution under Nakasone and DepCO Tighe. Rejects the prior "respond to incidents in own networks" posture. The doctrine asserts that cyberspace is a domain of constant interaction where ceding initiative cedes the terrain; therefore CYBERCOM must operate persistently in "red space" and "gray space" to impose costs and gather intelligence. Hunt Forward is the most visible operational expression.
- **Citations**: [1] CYBERCOM "Cyber 101" explainer. [2] 2018 Command Vision. [3] IBM Think / Michael P. Fischerkeller commentary. [4] War on the Rocks archive (Healey).

### Hunt Forward Operations

- **Issued**: operational concept since 2018; first publicly named deployment 2018 (Ukraine), publicly described by Nakasone 2019–22
- **Official text**: CYBERCOM news releases at https://www.cybercom.mil/Media/News/ — see "Hunt Forward Estonia" (2020), "Committed Partners in Cyberspace" Albania (2022). National Security Archive: "Before the Invasion: Hunt Forward Operations in Ukraine" (declassified 2022)
- **Status**: active; institutionalized in the CNMF (elevated to sub-unified command Dec 2022)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: alliance-based persistent engagement; intelligence harvest at the source (collect adversary tooling on partner networks before it reaches US); deterrence-by-denial signaling
- **Sectors / targets implied**: partner-government networks (defensive deployment); adversary actors found in those networks (typically GRU/FSB/MSS/MOIS)
- **Timeline pressure**: NATO eastern flank, election cycles in allied states, post-incident demands (Albania)
- **Summary**: CYBERCOM's flagship overt operation. CNMF deploys teams (typically 24–40 personnel) at invitation of a partner government to hunt for malicious activity on partner networks. By April 2024 Gen. Haugh confirmed 22 missions in 17 countries in 2023, planning 25 in 2025. Cumulatively 60+ deployments across 28 countries. Publicly named: Ukraine (2018, 2021–22), Estonia (2020), Lithuania (2020, 2024), Montenegro (2018, 2019), North Macedonia, Albania (2022 post-Iran attack), Latvia (2022), Croatia, Canada, Zambia, SOUTHCOM AOR (2024–25 — Chinese malware found on partner networks).
- **Citations**: [1] cybercom.mil press releases. [2] NSArchive "Before the Invasion" doc release. [3] Nakasone Senate Armed Services testimony 2022. [4] Gen. Haugh April 2024 posture statement. [5] CCDCOE "International Legal Framework for Hunt Forward" (Kosseff 2024).

### NSPM-13 (2018) — Cyber Operations Delegation

- **Issued**: August 2018 by President Trump (National Security Presidential Memorandum 13)
- **Official text**: classified; existence and effect documented in DoD IG, congressional testimony, and reporting
- **Status**: active (Biden retained with adjustments per 2022 review; Trump 2.0 preserved)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: delegates "well-defined" time-sensitive offensive cyber operations authority from POTUS to SecDef and onward to CYBERCOM; removes pre-2018 NSPD-20 interagency choke point
- **Summary**: The single most consequential US offensive-cyber policy of the past decade. NSPD-20 (Obama, 2012) had centralized cyber-op approval in the White House and the deconfliction process was, per John Bolton, "frozen solid." NSPM-13 enabled the operational tempo behind the 2018 IRA disruption, the ongoing Hunt Forward program, the 2020 Trickbot disruption, and Glowing Symphony's late phases. Biden's 2022 review modestly increased interagency consultation requirements but did not roll back the core delegation. Text remains classified; Congress received briefing docs in 2019 after 17-month delay.
- **Citations**: [1] CRS "Defense Primer: USCYBERCOM" (congress.gov). [2] CyberScoop reporting on NSPM-13 (Bing/Lyngaas). [3] John Bolton, *The Room Where It Happened*. [4] Lawfare "Biden Policy Changes for Offensive Cyber Operations." [5] FAS IRP National Security Presidential Memoranda index.

### Title 10 / Title 50 Authorities

- **Issued**: statutory framework predating cyber; explicit cyber updates in NDAA FY2018 §1633 (10 U.S.C. §394) and NDAA FY2019 §1642
- **Official text**: 10 U.S.C. §394 (https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title10-section394); 50 U.S.C. for IC authorities
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: Title 10 = DoD military operations (CYBERCOM); Title 50 = IC including covert action (CIA) and SIGINT (NSA). 10 U.S.C. §394 affirms that military cyber operations are "traditional military activities" — not covert action — and thus do not require presidential finding/Gang-of-Eight notification.
- **Summary**: The legal scaffolding under every US offensive cyber op. The Title 10/50 distinction matters operationally: CYBERCOM ops are TMA, subject to congressional armed-services oversight; CIA cyber ops are covert action under Title 50 §3093, requiring presidential finding and intelligence-committee notification. NSA SIGINT is Title 50 but not covert action. The dual-hat arrangement at Fort Meade lets a single 4-star wear both hats; this is the central reason for the long-running "split CYBERCOM and NSA" debate (revived 2025, shelved).
- **Citations**: [1] 10 U.S.C. §394. [2] CRS R45175 "Covert Action and Clandestine Activities." [3] Lawfare "Offensive Cyberspace Operations, the NDAA, and the Title 10–Title 50 Debate" (Chesney). [4] Hoover Aegis Paper "The Domestic Legal Framework for US Military Cyber Operations" (Chesney).

### Section 1642 NDAA FY2019 — Active Defense Authority

- **Issued**: signed August 13, 2018 as part of John S. McCain NDAA FY2019, §1642
- **Official text**: Public Law 115-232 §1642
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: standing authority for SecDef/CYBERCOM to "disrupt, defeat, and deter" active, systematic, ongoing cyberspace campaigns by Russia, China, North Korea, or Iran
- **Summary**: A congressional preauthorization. The National Command Authority (POTUS + SecDef) determines a state is engaged in a systematic campaign and unlocks Cyber Command's authority to respond proportionally in foreign cyberspace. Pairs with NSPM-13 to form the operational/legal stack behind CYBERCOM's disruption tempo against the "big four."
- **Citations**: [1] PL 115-232 §1642. [2] FAS analysis. [3] Lawfare "The Law of Military Cyber Operations and the New NDAA."

### Cyber Mission Force (organizational doctrine)

- **Issued**: established 2012; FOC declared May 2018; CNMF elevated to sub-unified command December 2022
- **Official text**: https://www.cybercom.mil/Media/News/Article/3206393/cyber-101-cyber-mission-force/
- **Status**: active; expanding (CMF growth from 133 to ~147 teams approved in FY24 budget)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: force-presentation model — Combat Mission Teams (CMTs) for combatant-command support, Cyber Protection Teams (CPTs) for DoDIN defense, National Mission Teams (NMTs) for homeland defense in cyberspace, plus Combat/National Support Teams (CSTs/NSTs)
- **Summary**: The organizational doctrine. CMF teams are the units that execute Defend Forward, Hunt Forward, and election-defense operations. NMTs under the CNMF run Hunt Forward and named operations like the IRA disruption; CMTs are aligned to geographic combatant commands; CPTs do the defensive work on DoD networks. Service-provided (ARCYBER, AFCYBER/16th AF, FLTCYBER/10th Fleet, MARFORCYBER, SPACECYBER) under OPCON to CYBERCOM.
- **Citations**: [1] CYBERCOM "Cyber 101" CMF explainer. [2] CRS IF13042 "Defense Primer: USCYBERCOM." [3] DoD Cyber Mission Analysis (PublicIntelligence).

### CISA Secure-by-Design (2023→)

- **Issued**: April 2023 initial guidance "Shifting the Balance of Cybersecurity Risk"; Secure-by-Design Pledge launched RSA May 2024
- **Official text**: https://www.cisa.gov/securebydesign; pledge at https://www.cisa.gov/securebydesign/pledge
- **Status**: active; 68+ major vendor signatories by mid-2024
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: vendor liability shift; 7 measurable pledge goals (MFA adoption, default-password elimination, reducing vulnerability classes, security patches, VDP, CVE accuracy, evidence of intrusion). Co-sealed with international partners (UK NCSC, Canada CCCS, Australia ACSC, Germany BSI, Netherlands NCSC, others).
- **Summary**: The operational expression of National Cybersecurity Strategy Pillar 3. Voluntary in name but coupled with procurement leverage (federal-contract requirements via EO 14028 and successor EOs) and a public scorecard. The 2025 EO 14306 rollbacks weakened some enforcement levers but did not retract the Pledge itself.
- **Citations**: [1] CISA Secure-by-Design landing page. [2] CISA pledge page. [3] CISA April 2024 announcement of 68 signatories. [4] Fortinet/Trend Micro etc. one-year reviews.

### ONCD Open-Source Software Security Initiative (OS3I)

- **Issued**: initiated 2022; 2023 RFI; January 2024 end-of-year report; August 2024 RFI summary
- **Official text**: https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/Securing-the-Open-Source-Software-Ecosystem-OS3I-End-of-Year-Report-MASTERCOPY.pdf
- **Status**: active under ONCD coordination
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: secure federal use of OSS; sustain OSS community investment; harmonize SBOM and memory-safe-language adoption; CISA OSS Roadmap (Feb 2024)
- **Summary**: Cross-agency (CISA, DARPA, DHS, NSF, NSA, GSA, OMB) effort to address the systemic-risk problem exposed by Log4j (2021) and XZ-Utils (2024). Twelve activities planned for 2024–25.
- **Citations**: [1] OS3I 2024 end-of-year report PDF. [2] ONCD fact sheet Jan 30, 2024. [3] CISA OSS Security Roadmap Feb 2024. [4] ONCD RFI summary Aug 9, 2024.

### Cyberspace Solarium Commission (2020) and CSC 2.0

- **Issued**: March 2020 final report; CSC 2.0 continues at McCrary Institute (Auburn)
- **Official text**: https://www.solarium.gov/report; CSC 2.0 annual implementation reports at https://cybersolarium.org/annual-assessment/
- **Status**: original commission sunset; CSC 2.0 advisory body active; ~79% of 82 recommendations implemented or in-flight as of 2024, with notable rollback in 2025 assessment
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: "layered cyber deterrence" — shape behavior, deny benefits, impose costs; structural reforms (ONCD creation, CISA empowerment, systemically important entities framework, Bureau of Cyber Statistics, Continuity of the Economy)
- **Summary**: The single most influential modern US cyber-policy blueprint. The 2021 NDAA created ONCD on Solarium's recommendation; CIRCIA, the National Cyber Director, the Joint Cyber Planning Office at CISA — all Solarium-traceable. The 2025 CSC 2.0 assessment flags "unprecedented setbacks" under Trump 2.0 staffing cuts to CISA and ONCD.
- **Citations**: [1] Solarium 2020 final report. [2] CSC 2.0 2024 Annual Report. [3] CSC 2.0 2025 Annual Report on Implementation. [4] Federal News Network coverage Sept 2024 and Oct 2025.

### IC Annual Threat Assessment — Cyber Chapter (annually, ODNI)

- **Issued**: annual since 2006; 2025 version released March 25, 2025
- **Official text**: https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/ATA-2025-Unclassified-Report.pdf
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: prioritizes PRC (Volt Typhoon, Salt Typhoon) as "most active and persistent" cyber threat; Russia for war-integration capability; Iran for opportunistic destructive ops; DPRK for revenue and WMD-support theft
- **Summary**: Public-facing IC consensus on the cyber threat landscape; reflects (and shapes) tasking priorities for the IC and CYBERCOM. The 2025 ATA elevated Salt Typhoon explicitly and reframed PRC pre-positioning as the headline state-cyber concern.
- **Citations**: [1] ATA 2025 unclassified PDF. [2] ATA 2024 unclassified PDF. [3] DNI Gabbard SSCI opening statement (March 2025).

### Dual-Hat NSA/CYBERCOM (structural doctrine)

- **Issued**: arrangement since CYBERCOM stand-up 2010; reaffirmed 2024 Biden review; separation shelved by Trump 2.0 in May 2025
- **Official text**: no single document; codified in DoDD 5100.01 and NSA mission statements
- **Status**: active
- **Summary**: A single 4-star (currently Gen. Tim Haugh, until his April 2025 firing; succeeded by Lt. Gen. Wilfred "Will" Hardy as acting) commands both NSA (Title 50) and CYBERCOM (Title 10). The structural rationale: speed of intelligence-to-operation. The structural critique: concentration of power, conflicts between SIGINT-stealth and operational-effect missions. The Trump administration's May 2025 decision to shelve separation has bipartisan congressional support per the relevant SASC/HASC chairs.
- **Citations**: [1] Lawfare "Two Hats Are Better Than Two Heads." [2] The Record May 2025 reporting. [3] DefenseScoop coverage. [4] Nakasone 2024 testimony.

### Five Eyes intelligence sharing (UKUSA Agreement)

- **Issued**: UKUSA 1946; cyber-era operationalization across Snowden-era documents
- **Status**: active
- **Summary**: Not a US-only doctrine, but the most important multilateral cyber-intelligence partnership the US participates in. AUS/CAN/NZ/UK/US share SIGINT, cyber threat intel, and (per Snowden documents) collection burdens including operations like Belgacom/Operation Socialist (GCHQ-led, US-benefiting). Joint advisories (CISA + NCSC + ACSC + CCCS + NCSC-NZ) are the standard format for major attribution announcements (Volt Typhoon, Salt Typhoon).
- **Citations**: [1] Declassified UKUSA Agreement (NSA FOIA). [2] Joint Five Eyes advisories at cisa.gov. [3] Snowden archive (Der Spiegel, The Intercept).
