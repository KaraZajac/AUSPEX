# Russia — Cyber-Relevant Strategic Doctrines

This file catalogs the formally published Kremlin doctrines and the informal but operationally-load-bearing strategic concepts that shape Russian cyber tasking. Cyber operations in the Russian model are subordinate to "information confrontation" (informatsionnoye protivoborstvo), which encompasses both technical-cyber and psychological-cognitive effects as a continuous, peacetime instrument of state power.

---

### Foreign Policy Concept of the Russian Federation (2023)

- **Issued**: 31 March 2023 by Presidential Decree No. 229; signed by Vladimir Putin, presented by Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov.
- **Official text**: https://mid.ru/en/foreign_policy/fundamental_documents/1860586/
- **Status**: active (supersedes 2016 Foreign Policy Concept)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - "Strengthening Russian sovereignty in the global information space" — i.e. control of the Runet and rejection of multistakeholder Internet governance.
  - Counter "unfriendly states" weaponizing the global information space; explicitly anti-US/NATO framing.
  - Push UN-led cyber norms regimes (Open-Ended Working Group, draft Convention on Information Crime) as alternative to Budapest Convention.
  - Civilizational/multipolar framing legitimizes operations in the "near abroad" and against "collective West."
- **Sectors / targets implied**: foreign foreign-ministries, election infrastructure of NATO states, Western media and think tanks, multilateral institutions, post-Soviet states still in the Western orbit (Moldova, Georgia, Baltics).
- **Timeline pressure**: codifies the post-February-2022 break with the West; treats sanctions regime as permanent.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The 2023 Concept formalizes Russia's self-positioning as a "civilization-state" leading a multipolar bloc against an "Anglo-Saxon"-led West. Section IV.55 lists ensuring "international information security" as a foreign-policy priority: shaping international law to legitimize sovereign segments of the Internet, opposing what Russia calls Western "weaponization" of cyberspace, and treating offensive Western cyber and influence operations as the principal external threat. This frame justifies pre-emptive and retaliatory Russian cyber operations as defensive sovereignty enforcement. Operationally it underwrites: SVR-led intelligence collection against MFAs and Western policy elites (Midnight Blizzard mailbox theft), GRU disruptive operations against NATO members supporting Ukraine, and FSB/GRU influence operations to fragment NATO consensus.
- **Citations**:
  1. MID, "The Concept of the Foreign Policy of the Russian Federation" (31 Mar 2023). https://mid.ru/en/foreign_policy/fundamental_documents/1860586/
  2. Russia Matters, "Russian Strategic Documents" archive. https://www.russiamatters.org/russian-strategic-documents

### Doctrine of Information Security of the Russian Federation (2016)

- **Issued**: 5 December 2016 by Presidential Decree No. 646; signed by Putin. Supersedes the 2000 Information Security Doctrine.
- **Official text**: http://en.kremlin.ru/acts/news/53418 ; PDF: https://afyonluoglu.org/PublicWebFiles/strategies/Asia/Russia%202016%20Information%20Security%20Doctrine.pdf
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Defends the "information sphere" as a national-security domain on par with land/sea/air/space.
  - Identifies foreign technical and psychological operations against Russian society — particularly through social networks and mass media — as principal threat.
  - Calls for state control over critical information infrastructure, domestic CII protection (later codified in Federal Law 187-FZ on Critical Information Infrastructure, 2017).
  - Mandates "neutralization of information-psychological impact aimed at undermining historical foundations and patriotic traditions."
- **Sectors / targets implied** (defensive scope, but read as offensive license): Russian state media, social platforms, anti-Western counter-narrative; offensively justifies kinetic-equivalent response to foreign info-ops.
- **Timeline pressure**: ratchets after the 2014 Maidan; preceded the Yarovaya laws and the 2019 "Sovereign Runet" law.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The 2016 doctrine is the canonical statement that Russia views information confrontation as continuous and existential. It elevates "information-psychological" threats (i.e. Western journalism, opposition civil society, foreign-funded NGOs) alongside technical cyber threats. Although nominally defensive, it is the doctrinal hook on which the Kremlin justifies offensive information operations as proportionate response: if the West is conducting information warfare against Russia through CNN and Twitter, then GRU psyops units and SVR influence cutouts are merely defensive. The doctrine also gave bureaucratic authority to FSB (Center 18 / Center for Information Security) and Roskomnadzor for domestic enforcement, and to the GRU's information units for external operations.
- **Citations**:
  1. Kremlin, "Information Security Doctrine approved" (6 Dec 2016). http://en.kremlin.ru/acts/news/53418
  2. FIIA, "Russia's New Information Security Doctrine" (2017). https://fiia.fi/en/publication/russias-new-information-security-doctrine
  3. Digital Watch Observatory archive. https://dig.watch/resource/doctrine-of-information-security-of-the-russian-federation

### National Security Strategy of the Russian Federation (2021)

- **Issued**: 2 July 2021 by Presidential Decree No. 400.
- **Official text**: English unofficial translation: https://rusmilsec.blog/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/nss_rf_2021_eng_.pdf
- **Status**: active (supersedes 2015 NSS)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Information security promoted to its own dedicated section (it was a subsection in 2015).
  - Calls for "forces and means of information confrontation" — i.e. an institutionalized offensive cyber/info capability.
  - Identifies foreign ICT use to "interfere in internal affairs, undermine sovereignty, violate territorial integrity" as a principal threat.
  - Endorses development of a "sovereign segment of the Internet" (Runet) and import substitution in IT/software.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: domestic ICT supply chain (defensive), foreign election and discourse infrastructure (offensive justification).
- **Timeline pressure**: pre-positions doctrine for the 2022 confrontation; provides retroactive legal cover for offensive operations.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The 2021 NSS elevates information confrontation to a top-tier national-security priority and explicitly authorizes building offensive capabilities. Whereas earlier documents framed cyber as defensive, the 2021 NSS sanctions "forces and means of information confrontation" — a euphemism that covers GRU disruptive units, SVR cyber-espionage, FSB collection, and the influence apparatus (Internet Research Agency successors, RT, Sputnik). Combined with the parallel push for digital sovereignty, the NSS frames Russia as both under siege and entitled to retaliatory information operations against the West and "color revolution" threats in the near abroad.
- **Citations**:
  1. Presidential Decree No. 400 of 2 July 2021 (English translation). https://rusmilsec.blog/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/nss_rf_2021_eng_.pdf
  2. Institut Montaigne, "Russia's National Security Strategy 2021: the Era of Information Confrontation." https://www.institutmontaigne.org/en/expressions/russias-national-security-strategy-2021-era-information-confrontation

### Military Doctrine of the Russian Federation (2014, amended 2020 implementing acts)

- **Issued**: 25 December 2014 by Presidential Decree No. Pr-2976 (supersedes 2010 Military Doctrine). Updated through subsequent National Defense Plan implementing acts.
- **Official text**: English summary: https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/world/russia/doctrine.htm ; ETH-CSS analytical text: https://www.files.ethz.ch/isn/192873/rp_117.pdf
- **Status**: active
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - First Russian doctrine to elevate "information confrontation" as a distinct form of warfare across peace and wartime.
  - Names NATO enlargement as the principal external military danger.
  - Identifies "destabilization of internal political and social situation" via information means as a military threat — i.e. cyber-enabled influence operations are now part of the military's mandate.
  - Authorizes pre-emptive use of forces, including in "non-contact" warfare, where cyber operations are doctrinally fit.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: NATO C4ISR, allied-state political stability, electoral infrastructure, military logistics, defense industrial base.
- **Timeline pressure**: codified after Crimea annexation; supports continuous gray-zone operations.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The 2014 Military Doctrine is the formal text that put cyber/information operations under General Staff purview alongside conventional forces. It identifies NATO and Western "indirect actions" (color revolutions, information operations, support for opposition) as the principal military threat, justifying continuous Russian counter-operations below the threshold of armed conflict. Read together with subsequent GRU activity, it frames Sandworm, Fancy Bear, and the GRU's information-psychological warfare units (Unit 54777) as legitimate military instruments. The doctrine's emphasis on "non-contact" and "indirect" warfare is the bureaucratic source for what Western analysts later mislabeled the "Gerasimov doctrine."
- **Citations**:
  1. RAND, "Russia's New Military Doctrine: Same as the Old Doctrine, Mostly" (Jan 2015). https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2015/01/russias-new-military-doctrine-same-as-the-old-doctrine.html
  2. ETH-CSS, "Russia's 2014 Military Doctrine and Beyond." https://www.files.ethz.ch/isn/192873/rp_117.pdf
  3. European Parliament, "Russia's national security strategy and military doctrine" (2017). https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/IDAN/2017/578016/EXPO_IDA(2017)578016_EN.pdf

### "Gerasimov Framework" on Hybrid / Non-Linear Warfare (2013, informal)

- **Issued**: 27 February 2013 by Gen. Valery Gerasimov, Chief of the General Staff, in Voyenno-Promyshlennyy Kurier (Military-Industrial Courier).
- **Original text** (Russian): https://www.vpk-news.ru/articles/14632 ; English translation by Robert Coalson widely reprinted.
- **Status**: inferred (informal; not a published doctrine — but operationally cited)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Asserts that "the rules of war have changed" — non-military means achieve political-strategic goals at 4:1 ratio over conventional force.
  - Identifies information confrontation, cyber, special operations, and proxy forces as the modern operational mix.
  - Treats color revolutions as a form of warfare initiated by the West, which Russia must mirror.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: pre-conflict shaping of target-state population; combination of cyber, psyops, financial coercion, and special operations.
- **Timeline pressure**: pre-Crimea articulation; arguably descriptive of US/Western methods more than prescriptive of Russian doctrine — but cited by Russian officials and Western analysts alike.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The Gerasimov article is informal — Mark Galeotti, who coined "Gerasimov doctrine," has since retracted the label — but it remains the most-cited Russian-authored framing of integrated hybrid warfare. Whether or not it is a formal doctrine, it captures the operational pattern: GRU cyber units, GRU/SVR influence operations, FSB "active measures," PMC Wagner-style proxies, energy coercion, and conventional escalation all operate as a unified gray-zone toolkit. For AUSPEX tagging, the Gerasimov framing is useful as the proximate articulation of "hybrid warfare" doctrine but should not be cited as an official Kremlin text; cite the 2014 Military Doctrine for the formal authority and the 2021 NSS for current emphasis.
- **Citations**:
  1. Gerasimov, "Tsennost' nauki v predvidenii" (Value of Science in Foresight), VPK No. 8 (476), 27 Feb 2013. https://www.vpk-news.ru/articles/14632
  2. CEPA, "The Evolution of Russian Hybrid Warfare." https://cepa.org/comprehensive-reports/the-evolution-of-russian-hybrid-warfare-executive-summary/
  3. Mark Galeotti, "I'm Sorry for Creating the 'Gerasimov Doctrine'" (Foreign Policy, Mar 2018).

### "Russian World" (Russkiy Mir) / Near Abroad / Sphere of Influence

- **Issued**: institutionalized via Russkiy Mir Foundation (Presidential Decree No. 796, 21 Jun 2007); reinforced in 2014 Crimea-annexation speeches and 2022 invasion justifications.
- **Official text**: https://russkiymir.ru/en/fund/index.php (foundation); Putin Mar 2014 Crimea address; Sep 2022 annexation address.
- **Status**: active (operative quasi-ideology)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Justifies operations to "protect compatriots" and Russian-speakers in post-Soviet states.
  - Underwrites continuous influence and intelligence operations in Ukraine, Belarus, Moldova, Baltics, Kazakhstan, Georgia, Armenia.
  - Legitimizes Russian Orthodox Church as a soft-power and intelligence-collection vector.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: post-Soviet government and security ministries, language/education NGOs in target states, diaspora networks, Russian-speaking media.
- **Timeline pressure**: continuous; accelerated post-2014 and again post-2022.
- **Summary** (~150 words): Russkiy Mir is not a published cyber doctrine but is the civilizational frame that makes the near abroad the priority cyber theater. Gamaredon's seven-plus-year campaign against Ukrainian government networks, the long FSB cultivation of Moldovan and Georgian political assets, and Sandworm's pre-positioning in Baltic critical infrastructure are all coherent only through this lens. AUSPEX should treat any cyber operation against Ukraine, Belarus, Moldova, Georgia, Armenia, Kazakhstan, or the Baltics as presumptively Russkiy-Mir-aligned unless evidence indicates otherwise.
- **Citations**:

  2. Wikipedia (well-sourced overview), "Russian world." https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_world

### NATO Non-Enlargement / December 2021 Ultimatum

- **Issued**: 17 December 2021 by MID — two draft treaties: "Treaty between the USA and Russia on Security Guarantees" and "Agreement on Measures to Ensure the Security of Russia and Member States of NATO."
- **Official text**: https://www.mid.ru/en/foreign_policy/news/1790809/
- **Status**: active demand (formally rejected by NATO/US Jan 2022, but remains a stated Russian war aim)
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Codifies NATO enlargement as a casus belli — direct cyber-targeting rationale for any aspirant or new member.
  - Authorizes (in Russian framing) preventive operations against US/NATO infrastructure in Europe.
  - Demands withdrawal of US forces and infrastructure from post-1997 NATO members.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: NATO command/logistics, defense industrial base of new members (Finland, Sweden post-2023/24 accession), Ukraine/Georgia partner-program infrastructure.
- **Timeline pressure**: codified just before Feb 2022 invasion; persists as the strategic justification for ongoing operations.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The December 2021 draft treaties are the clearest statement of Russia's geopolitical war aim — reversing NATO's post-1997 expansion and securing a veto over allied alignment in the post-Soviet space. The demands were rejected, and the invasion followed. For AUSPEX, this is the doctrinal source for tagging Russian operations against NATO defense supply chains, Ukrainian C2 and grid infrastructure, Finnish/Swedish post-accession targets, and US/UK defense contractors as serving the strategic goal of degrading NATO cohesion and Ukraine support. Viasat KA-SAT, NotPetya in retrospect, and the post-2022 wiper campaigns all map cleanly here.
- **Citations**:
  1. MID, "Press release on Russian draft documents on legal security guarantees" (17 Dec 2021). https://www.mid.ru/en/foreign_policy/news/1790809/
  2. Brookings, "Russia's draft agreements with NATO and the United States: Intended for rejection?" https://www.brookings.edu/articles/russias-draft-agreements-with-nato-and-the-united-states-intended-for-rejection/

### Energy Weaponization (operational pattern, not formal doctrine)

- **Issued**: doctrinally implicit; observable from 1990 Baltic oil cutoffs, 2006/2009 Ukraine gas wars, 2014 onward, peaking with Nord Stream curtailments and 2022 supply cuts.
- **Status**: active operational pattern; not codified in single text
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Pipeline and grid operations as coercive lever paired with cyber pre-positioning in target-country energy infrastructure.
  - Sandworm operations against Ukrainian energy grids (2015, 2016, 2022, 2023) align with kinetic missile strikes on the same grid.
  - Berserk Bear (FSB Center 16) long-running campaign against US/UK/EU energy-sector ICS networks (CISA Mar 2018, DOJ Mar 2022 indictment).
- **Sectors / targets implied**: power generation/transmission, oil and gas, LNG terminals, refineries, nuclear plants.
- **Timeline pressure**: tied to seasonal pressure (winter heating in Europe) and the EU energy diversification timeline.
- **Summary** (~150 words): The "energy weapon" is the operational expression of Russia's understanding that hydrocarbon supply and electric-grid availability are political instruments. Cyber operations complement kinetic and market levers: Sandworm's repeated power-grid strikes in Ukraine are timed with military operations; Berserk Bear's pre-positioning in US/UK/EU energy ICS environments is the cyber analogue to Nord Stream coercion. For AUSPEX, any Russian intrusion in an energy-sector target should be tagged against this strand even absent immediate disruptive intent — the strategic goal is coercive leverage.

### Post-2022 Sanctions-Response Posture (informal but visible)

- **Issued**: not formally published as a doctrine; reconstructed from Putin speeches, MID statements, and observed operational tempo since Feb 2022.
- **Status**: active operational posture
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Treat Western sanctions as economic warfare warranting cyber response.
  - Mobilize criminal proxies (Conti, Killnet, NoName057(16), CARR) as deniable retaliatory force.
  - Prioritize operations against states providing Ukraine military aid (Poland, Baltic states, Germany, UK, US).
  - Disrupt sanctions-evasion-relevant chokepoints and erode Western public support for Ukraine.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: financial sector (SWIFT-adjacent banks, sanctions-enforcement agencies), defense logistics, election infrastructure in aid-providing states, public-facing government services for DDoS/morale ops.
- **Timeline pressure**: tracks Ukraine aid bills (US supplementals, Ramstein meetings), election cycles in NATO states.
- **Summary** (~150 words): After February 2022, Russia's cyber program shifted from primarily strategic intelligence collection and limited disruption toward a broader retaliatory posture incorporating criminal proxies. The Conti gang's public pledge to defend the Russian state, Killnet's coordinated DDoS against NATO governments, NoName057(16)'s 1,500+ DDoS attacks against Ukraine-aligned states, and the Cyber Army of Russia Reborn's manipulation of US water-utility HMIs (with subsequent Treasury attribution to GRU Unit 74455) all show a deliberate state-criminal-hacktivist fusion. The doctrine is not written down, but the pattern is consistent enough — and Treasury/DOJ public attributions are explicit enough — to treat as a working strategic frame.
