# DPRK doctrines — cyber-relevant

Strategic doctrines and ideological pillars that shape DPRK cyber tasking. Listed roughly in order of current operational relevance.

---

### Juche (self-reliance)

- **Issued**: First articulated by Kim Il Sung in December 1955 ("On Eliminating Dogmatism and Formalism and Establishing Juche in Ideological Work"); codified in the DPRK Constitution (1972, amended 1992, 1998, 2009, 2016, 2019); declared the sole guiding ideology of the WPK in 1980.
- **Official text**: DPRK 1972/2016 Constitution preamble and Article 3; Kim Jong Il's *On the Juche Idea* (1982).
- **Status**: active — foundational ideology, not subject to revision.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - *jaju* (political-ideological independence) → justifies operating outside the international financial order
  - *jarip* (economic self-sufficiency) → cover-rationale for sanctions evasion as a national-survival imperative; cyber theft framed internally as "self-reliance" not "crime"
  - *jawi* (military independence) → indigenous offensive-cyber capability as a non-imported strategic asset
- **Sectors / targets implied**: targets-of-opportunity globally; no geographic restraint norm.
- **Timeline pressure**: none — doctrinal constant.
- **Summary** (~150 words): Juche is the bedrock political theology of the DPRK regime and is the ideological permission slip for the entire DPRK cyber program. Because Juche frames the DPRK as the unique sovereign master of its own fate, sanctions imposed by hostile powers are treated not as legitimate legal constraints to be evaded surreptitiously but as imperialist hostility to be defeated by *any* effective means. This frees the regime to industrialize cyber theft as a normal organ of state finance — Office 39, the RGB cyber units, and the IT-worker scheme are all defended internally as expressions of *jarip* (economic self-reliance) under hostile conditions. Operationally, Juche also predicts a high tolerance for reputational cost: DPRK rarely uses false-flag tradecraft, because the regime treats public attribution of cyber operations as politically neutral or even positive.
- **Citations**: [1] DPRK Constitution; [2] Britannica Juche entry; [3] Wikipedia Juche.

### Songun (military-first politics)

- **Issued**: First publicly named in *Rodong Sinmun*, 7 April 1997, under Kim Jong Il. Effective from late 1990s.
- **Status**: **superseded / de-emphasized**. In late 2021 Kim Jong Un publicly replaced "Songun" with "*inmin daejung jeil juui*" ("people-first politics") as the operative slogan. The institutional residue, however — the KPA, the RGB, and military-intel cyber units — remains fully intact and well-resourced.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: military and military-intelligence services receive priority budget allocation, manpower, and political backing. The RGB cyber portfolio is a direct beneficiary.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: ROK/US/JP military, defense contractors, sanctions-enforcement targets.
- **Timeline pressure**: none current; legacy doctrine.
- **Summary** (~150 words): Songun is the ideological scaffolding that built the modern DPRK cyber program. Under Kim Jong Il, the KPA and the intelligence services were elevated above the Party in resource priority, and the consolidation of cyber units under the RGB in 2009 occurred under explicit Songun logic. Kim Jong Un has rhetorically retired Songun in favor of "people-first politics," but the cyber-organizational consequences are permanent — the RGB remains the cyber-owning service, and military-intel tasking dominates DPRK targeting. AUSPEX should treat Songun as historically explanatory (why the RGB owns cyber) rather than currently operative (it does not generate new tasking).
- **Citations**: [4] Wikipedia Songun; [5] *Ideology of the Workers' Party of Korea* (Wikipedia, citing Rodong Sinmun); [6] Kim Jong Un 2021 8th Party Congress address.

### Byungjin (parallel nuclear + economic development)

- **Issued**: 31 March 2013, declared at Plenary Meeting of the WPK Central Committee under Kim Jong Un. Formally declared **complete and superseded** in April 2018 at the 3rd Plenary Meeting of the 7th WPK Central Committee, when Kim announced the nuclear deterrent was achieved and the line would now pivot to "socialist economic construction."
- **Official text**: Kim Jong Un's March 2013 Plenary report; April 2018 Plenary report.
- **Status**: **superseded April 2018**, but its operational footprint persists — particularly the institutional bias that cyber theft is the natural fundraising mechanism for nuclear advancement.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Simultaneous pursuit of nuclear deterrent + civilian economy; cyber theft scaled in this window (2013–2018) because conventional foreign-exchange streams could not finance both simultaneously.
  - Many of the canonical DPRK cyber operations — DarkSeoul, Sony, Bangladesh Bank, WannaCry — fall inside the Byungjin window.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: dual-use — banking/SWIFT for revenue, defense/aerospace for technology, broad disruption for political signaling.
- **Timeline pressure**: window closed 2018.
- **Summary** (~150 words): Byungjin (also romanized *byongjin*, *pyongjin*) is the doctrinal frame under which the DPRK cyber program scaled from a regional nuisance to a global threat. It explicitly required parallel funding of nuclear weapons and economic development at a time when external aid had collapsed. The Byungjin window (2013–2018) is the institutional birthplace of APT38's SWIFT campaign and the early Lazarus crypto theft. Kim Jong Un declared the doctrine complete in April 2018 once the *Hwasong-15* ICBM test was assessed sufficient, and the regime line pivoted to "concentration on economic construction." That pivot did not last — the 2019 Hanoi summit collapse and 2020 COVID-shock returned the regime to active modernization, formalized at the 2021 8th Party Congress.
- **Citations**: [7] GlobalSecurity.org Byungjin; [8] *Byungjin and the Logic of Authoritarian Survival* (Lee, SNU); [9] Wikipedia WPK ideology.

### Post-2018 Nuclear-Force Modernization Line / 8th Party Congress Five-Year Defense Plan (2021–2025)

- **Issued**: Kim Jong Un's report to the 8th Congress of the Workers' Party of Korea, 5–12 January 2021, Pyongyang. Reaffirmed and extended at the 9th Party Congress preparatory plenums (2024–2025).
- **Official text**: KCNA English transcript of Kim's report (Jan 9, 2021); reproduced in NK News and 38 North translations.
- **Status**: **active and currently dominant**. This is the doctrinal driver for nearly all 2021–2026 DPRK cyber tasking.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars** — the 13 new weapon systems Kim explicitly enumerated:
  1. Tactical nuclear weapons (miniaturized warheads)
  2. Super-large hydrogen warheads (boosted yield)
  3. Solid-fuel ICBMs (since realized as *Hwasong-18*, *Hwasong-19*)
  4. ICBMs with MIRV capability
  5. ICBMs with range 15,000 km
  6. Hypersonic glide vehicles (since realized as *Hwasong-8*, *Hwasong-16B*)
  7. Nuclear-powered ballistic-missile submarine (SSBN program)
  8. Submarine-launched ballistic missiles (SLBM, including solid-fuel)
  9. Military reconnaissance satellites (Malligyong-1 launched Nov 2023)
  10. Unmanned aerial reconnaissance assets (5,000 km radius)
  11. Underwater nuclear-armed drones (*Haeil*)
  12. Advanced warhead guidance & re-entry
  13. Tactical battlefield nuclear delivery (*Hwasan-31* warhead, 2023)
- **Sectors / targets implied**: hard-currency aggregation (crypto exchanges, DeFi, IT-worker placement) at maximum tempo to finance the 13-system program; targeted technical-intelligence collection against aerospace, missile-defense, submarine quieting tech, satellite imagery and ground-systems vendors; ROK defense industrial base; NASA OIG and US defense contractors per Andariel indictment.
- **Timeline pressure**: original 5-year plan window 2021–2025; 38 North (Jan 2026) and US-IC assessments find roughly half the 13 systems demonstrated by deadline, the rest extended into a successor program likely formalized at the 9th Party Congress.
- **Summary** (~150 words): This is the single most operationally relevant doctrine for AUSPEX's DPRK targeting model. Every dollar stolen by TraderTraitor and every dollar wage-fraudulently collected by a DPRK IT worker is, per Treasury's standardized OFAC language, "support for the DPRK's WMD and ballistic-missile programs" — which means the 13-system list. The five-year defense plan is essentially the procurement requirement that the RGB cyber portfolio is tasked to underwrite. Crypto-theft tempo correlates tightly with missile-test tempo: 2022, 2023, 2024 and 2025 each saw record-or-near-record annual theft totals in lockstep with accelerating test schedules. Defense-contractor intrusions by Andariel target the *technical* dimension of the same list (warhead miniaturization, submarine quieting, satellite payloads).
- **Citations**: [10] Open Nuclear Network analysis of 8th Congress; [11] 38 North (Jan 2026) *Assessing North Korea's Five-Year Effort to Develop 13 New Nuclear and Missile Systems*; [12] CRS IF10472 (May 2025 update); [13] USNI News congressional report May 2025.

### Cyber as primary asymmetric force-generation and hard-currency mechanism (operational doctrine, inferred)

- **Issued**: not formally published. Operationalized progressively from ~2014 (Sony) through 2017 (WannaCry) and institutionalized post-2018 once crypto became viable.
- **Status**: **inferred — not formally published**, but consistently attested in primary-source language. Treasury press releases (sb0302 March 2023; sb0416 May 2024; sb0230 March 2024) state that DPRK cyber theft generates "**hundreds of millions of dollars**" or "**nearly $800 million in 2024 alone**" used to "**fund the DPRK's WMD and ballistic-missile programs**." The UN 1718 PoE March 2024 final report estimated **up to half** of DPRK foreign-currency income now comes from cyber theft.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**:
  - Cyber theft replaces and exceeds traditional Office 39 illicit revenue streams (counterfeiting, narcotics, arms sales, restaurant workers, coal exports).
  - Per-incident yield-to-risk ratio massively favors cyber over kinetic illicit operations.
  - Cyber is the *only* sanctions-evasion vector that scales sublinearly with sanctions tightening — every additional sanction on DPRK banking pushes more revenue onto the cyber channel.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: cryptocurrency exchanges and custodians, DeFi bridges, Web3 supply chain (npm, PyPI, JumpCloud, 3CX, Safe{Wallet}), smaller-tier banks on the SWIFT network (historical), online gambling, NFT platforms.
- **Timeline pressure**: matches the nuclear-modernization line.
- **Summary** (~150 words): This is the unwritten but absolutely central doctrine of contemporary DPRK statecraft. While Pyongyang has never published a "cyber doctrine" white paper, US Treasury, the FBI, the UN PoE, and ROK's NIS have collectively documented that cyber theft is now the dominant single revenue stream financing the Workers' Party's strategic procurement. Chainalysis cumulative tracking puts DPRK crypto theft at $6.75B lower-bound by end of 2025; Treasury and UN PoE characterize this revenue as **directly funding WMD and ballistic-missile development**. The doctrinal innovation is treating cyber not as a tool of war but as a tool of *finance* — a primary instrument of the regime's procurement budget. AUSPEX should treat this as the master doctrine: under it, every actor cluster (Lazarus core, APT38, TraderTraitor, Andariel ransomware, IT-worker fraud) is a revenue-channel diversification strategy.
- **Citations**: [14] Treasury sb0416 (May 2024); [15] Treasury sb0302 (Mar 2023); [16] UN 1718 PoE final report Mar 2024; [17] Chainalysis 2025 stolen-funds report; [18] CISA AA23-040A.

### Sanctions-evasion-as-state-policy (Office 39 financial backbone)

- **Issued**: not a published doctrine; operationalized through Central Committee Bureau 39 (Office 39 / Room 39) of the WPK, established in the late 1970s by Kim Jong Il.
- **Status**: **active**. Office 39 has progressively absorbed cyber revenue streams as legacy revenue (supernotes counterfeiting, narcotics, insurance fraud) declined.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: cyber-theft proceeds are laundered through Office 39's overseas front-company network (heavily PRC- and Southeast-Asia-based), then disbursed to procurement channels and elite-loyalty payments.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: cyber operators (RGB) are the *generators*; Office 39 is the *aggregator*. Money-mule infrastructure spans OTC crypto desks (predominantly PRC-affiliated), shell trading companies, embassy diplomatic-pouch channels.
- **Timeline pressure**: continuous; intensifies under each new UN/US sanctions tightening.
- **Summary** (~150 words): Office 39 is the financial back-of-house that turns RGB cyber-theft *output* into regime *spending*. Estimated annual throughput historically $500M–$1B+, now likely several times higher given cyber-theft scale. It runs a global network of front companies (concentrated in PRC border regions and Southeast Asia), money-launderers, and OTC desks. From an AUSPEX perspective, Office 39 is not a cyber actor but is the doctrinal "purpose" downstream of every financial-theft event — its existence is what makes the strategic-goal attribution "support DPRK WMD program" mechanically true. Treasury sb0302 (March 2023) sanctioned multiple Office 39-affiliated bankers in the UAE, China, and Russia explicitly for laundering DPRK cybercrime and IT-worker proceeds.
- **Citations**: [19] Treasury sb0302; [20] CRDF Global Office 39 briefing; [21] Time / Ri Jong Ho defector testimony; [22] Wikipedia Room 39.

### Strategic-information and political-defection operations (inferred from MSS/APT37 + RGB/Kimsuky tasking)

- **Issued**: inferred from sustained targeting pattern, 2012–present.
- **Status**: active.
- **Cyber-relevant pillars**: surveillance of defectors, dissident journalists, NK-focused researchers, ROK and US foreign-policy think tanks; collection on inter-Korean dialogue, US Korea-policy planning, sanctions-enforcement coordination; pre-emption of dissent and recovery of dissidents/defectors.
- **Sectors / targets implied**: ROK Ministry of Unification, ROK NIS-affiliated researchers, Korea-watcher journalists (NK News, Daily NK, *Chosun Ilbo*), US think tanks (CSIS, Brookings, Wilson Center, Heritage, Atlantic Council), human-rights NGOs.
- **Timeline pressure**: continuous, intensifies around inter-Korean and US-DPRK diplomatic windows.
- **Summary** (~150 words): A subset of DPRK cyber tasking serves classic Juche internal-security objectives — surveillance of those who threaten the regime narrative, whether they are defectors, journalists, or foreign policy researchers shaping adversary positions on Pyongyang. APT37 (MSS-attributed) is the cleanest case: persistent targeting of defectors, journalists covering DPRK, and ROK and Japanese policy entities, distinguished by limited financial motivation. Kimsuky / APT43 overlaps the same target set but with broader RGB-aligned collection requirements including nuclear and sanctions policy. The Mandiant March 2023 APT43 report uniquely documented that this cluster *self-funds* through small-scale cybercrime to purchase operational infrastructure — a doctrinal innovation that decouples espionage tasking from centrally allocated budget.
- **Citations**: [23] Mandiant APT43 report Mar 2023; [24] Kaspersky ScarCruft research; [25] Volexity InkySquid (2021).

## Notes on doctrinal sourcing

- DPRK publishes very little public doctrine in the Western sense. The 8th Party Congress report is the closest available official document; everything else is reconstructed from KCNA reporting of plenum decisions, defector accounts (Ri Jong Ho on Office 39 being the canonical one), and counterparty attribution (US Treasury OFAC language about WMD/missile financing).
- The "cyber doctrine" of the DPRK is therefore a *reconstruction* — strongly attested by US/UN financial-flow tracking but never formally promulgated by Pyongyang. AUSPEX should preserve this distinction in confidence labels.
